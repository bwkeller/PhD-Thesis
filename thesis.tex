    \begin{abstract}
        \addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
        \setcounter{page}{3}\thispagestyle{plain}
            Galaxy formation is a complex, nonlinear process that occurs over
            scales that span many orders of magnitude in both space and time.
            Galaxy simulations will always be unable to resolve phenomena that
            occur on scales below resolution limits.  Of these phenomena,
            supernovae from massive stars are among the most important.  For
            decades, simulations have failed to reproduce galaxies like our own
            Milky Way.  Simulated galaxies produced too many stars too close to
            the centre of the galaxy.  Failing to accurately model the effects
            of supernovae has long been suspected to be the cause of this
            problem.  In this thesis, we present a new model for supernovae
            feedback that captures the effects of previously ignored physics:
            thermal conduction in superbubbles.  Massive stars form in clusters,
            allowing their supernovae ejecta merge into a superbubble, which can
            vent from the disc to drive a galactic outflow. Thermal conduction
            acts to regulate how much mass is mixed into the superbubble.  We
            show that this model is robust to varying resolution and unresolved
            structure, allowing it to be used without expensive and potentially
            biasing parameter tuning.

            We use this new model to study the formation of galaxies like our
            own, and come to four major conclusions. First, we find that
            superbubbles drive significantly stronger galactic outflows of gas
            early in the history of the universe compared to past models of
            supernovae feedback.  Second, these outflows are key to both
            preventing the overproduction of stars and the formation of
            too-massive central bulges.  This comes about by the ejection of
            starforming gas, as well as the preferential removal of bulge
            forming gas in high redshift outflows.  Third, we show that
            supernovae cannot prevent runaway star formation in galaxies more
            massive than our own $(M_{halo}>10^{12}\;\rm{M_\odot})$.  This
            occurs hand in hand with funnelling of gas towards the centre of the
            galaxy, where supermassive black holes have been observed.
            Together, this provides a strong theoretical argument for a
            transition between regulation from stars to regulation from
            supermassive black holes at roughly this mass.  Finally, we use our
            simulated galaxies to show that recent observations of the radial
            acceleration relation are consistent with $\Lambda$CDM cosmology.
            The radial acceleration relation ties galaxy kinematics to baryonic
            mass, and new observations have suggested a tight, universal radial
            acceleration relation exists.  While this has been claimed to be
            evidence of exotic new physics, this same tight relation occurs for
            galaxies formed in concordance cosmology model.
        

    \end{abstract}
